### ✅ **1. Glue Job – Schema Evolution**

**Q:** *“In your Glue job written in PySpark, how did you handle schema evolution if a new column was added in the S3 CSV file?”*

**Answer:**

> “I used `.option("inferSchema", "true")` while reading the file in PySpark.
> Also, I enabled **‘schema evolution’ in Glue job settings** and **used Hudi** for versioned writes.
> This helped Glue automatically detect and adjust when new columns were added — without breaking the pipeline.”

---

### ✅ **2. Partitioning in Parquet**

**Q:** *“How did you choose the partition key, and what happens if partitioning is not optimal?”*

**Answer:**

> “I used high-cardinality fields like `load_date` or `region_id` to partition, depending on query patterns.
> If partitioning is not optimal, queries can become slow because Athena or Glue has to scan too many small files or read unnecessary partitions.
> So, we balance between too many and too few partitions — keeping partition size around 128–256 MB.”

---

### ✅ **3. Data Deduplication in ETL**

**Q:** *“How did you ensure no duplicate records were written to the fact table?”*

**Answer:**

> “I used the **Hudi upsert feature** and specified a **primary key column** like `transaction_id`.
> In PySpark, I also used `dropDuplicates()` before writing.
> And in some cases, I used **window functions** to keep the latest record based on `last_updated_date`.”

---

### ✅ **4. Redshift COPY Best Practices**

**Q:** *“What are the best practices you followed while using COPY command from S3 to Redshift?”*

**Answer:**

> “I followed these best practices:
>
> * Compressed files in **Parquet or GZIP**
> * Enabled `COMPUPDATE OFF` and `STATUPDATE OFF` for faster loads
> * Loaded data into **staging tables** first, then moved to fact tables after deduplication
> * Used IAM roles with limited S3 access and **manifest files** for atomic loads.”

---

### ✅ **5. Athena Optimization**

**Q:** *“How did you optimize performance and reduce costs when querying large data in Athena?”*

**Answer:**

> “I used **Parquet format** with **partitioned folders** (like year/month/day) to reduce scanned data.
> I always **used `SELECT only needed columns`** instead of `SELECT *`.
> Also, I created **Glue crawlers** to keep metadata updated and **used CTAS (Create Table As Select)** queries for temp aggregations.”

### ✅ **2. Error Handling in Glue Jobs**

**Q:** *“If your Glue job fails halfway during transformation, how do you recover from that? What retry or alerting mechanisms do you put in place?”*

**Answer:**

> “I build my jobs to be **idempotent**, so if they fail, I can re-run them without duplicate data.
> For retry, I use Glue job parameters and CloudWatch alarms.
> If the job fails, it triggers an **SNS alert to email/Slack**.
> I also log each stage (read, transform, write), so I can pinpoint the failure easily.”

### ✅ **3. Redshift Model Design**

**Q:** *“How did you model your Redshift tables — did you use star schema or snowflake schema? And why?”*

**Answer:**

> “I used the **star schema** mostly, because it’s simple and faster for analytics.
> We created one fact table for transactions and linked it to dimension tables like customers, products, and regions using surrogate keys.
> This reduced joins, improved performance, and made it easier for BI teams to build dashboards.”
